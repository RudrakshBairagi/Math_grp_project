01 / Problem Understanding

The Real-World Situation

A hospital emergency room experiences varying patient arrivals throughout the day. During peak hours, the influx can fluctuate unpredictably.

The Administration Needs to Know:

  • How often do extreme influxes occur?
  • How should the hospital prepare staffing levels to handle these surges without overstaffing during normal times?
02 / The Main Uncertainty

What exactly is random or varying?

The Event

The exact number of patients walking through the Emergency Room doors in any given 1-hour window.

The Random Variable (X)

The total count of patients arriving per hour during peak times.

Why it matters:

If arrivals are too high, patients face critical delays and doctors are overwhelmed. If too low, hospital resources are underutilized.

03 / Data Used (Simulated)

30-Day Simulated Influx Data

Generated simulated data representing standard "peak hour" shifts.
Sample Mean: ~14.1 patients/hour.
Theoretical Baseline: λ = 15 patients/hour.

04 / Mathematical Model

The Poisson Distribution

Why we chose this model:

  1. Predicts probability of a given number of events (arrivals) in a fixed interval (1 hour).
  2. Patients arrive independently of one another.
  3. The average rate (λ = 15) is constant and known.
Formula Used
P(X = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^k}{k!}

X = Target number of arrivals
λ = Average arrival rate (15)

06 / Interpretation

What Do These Numbers Mean?

✓

Routine Staffing is Highly Effective

~85% of the time, patient load stays in the comfortable range. Current baseline models work perfectly here.

!

Surges are Rare but Statistically Certain

A true crisis (25+ patients) happens ~1.12% of the time. This means it will predictably occur about once every 90 peak hours.

Conclusion: Treating an extreme scenario as an unexpected anomaly is dangerous; statistically, it is guaranteed to happen and must be planned for.

07 / Final Recommendation

Strategic Action Plan

1

Optimize the Baseline

Staff the ER to comfortably handle up to 20 continuous arrivals an hour. Our calculations prove this effortlessly covers >91% of all scenarios.

2

Dynamic "Surge" Protocol

Implement an "On-Call" surge rotation. When the ER registers an early leading indicator (like 10 arrivals in 20 minutes), immediately activate backup personnel.
